Indeed Connect

Adding a VIP experience for both employers and job seekers.

A bit of context

Indeed Connect came about as a way to fundamentally rethink how Indeed worked, tailoring an experience that focused more on conversations, connecting the right people, and less on formal applications.

To help put all this into better perspective, let's start with looking at how Indeed functions today.

About Indeed

Indeed today is an open platform. Jobs can be posted directly to the site, with additional jobs also getting aggregated from different sources for a "one stop job hunting shop". As a jobseeker, you can go and search for any job, in any location and depending on the job, apply even without having created an Indeed account.

For employers, the bar isn't much higher. The basic employer journey over a job's lifecycle looks something like this.

The only real requirements for a job post are a title, location and a description longer than a few words. We prompt you for additional information such as required licenses etc... where applicable, and try to upsell you for paid services along the way, but these pieces of information are optional and posting a job will always be free.

For both employers and candidates, the main way to connect is through submitting a (sometimes lengthy) formal application, at which point, employers have the opportunity to reach out.

Indeed Connect flips all this on it's head by doing the following:

Both employers and job seekers need to have a dedicated Indeed account with a verified identity to participate.

During signup, jobseekers go through an onboarding process where they provide additional information about their work, education history and  job preferences.

For employers, in addition to account verification, we also collect structured data about the job to help them build better, more descriptive job posts, and to also help facilitate better matching between both parties.

This is where a lot of the magic happens. For jobseekers, after opting in to the program they are automatically (and only) shown jobs that are a good potential fit. And in place of  a formal, lengthy application process, jobseekers can indicate interest in a job with a single tap, or message an employer directly.

Though having candidates message you directly can sound intimidating to employers, our matching ensures that any candidate that reaches out to is going to be a reasonably good match for the job.

Employers however, have to respond to all interested candidates in a timely manner. If the employer's response time goes past 3 days, we put their job on hold until they get caught up.

So what's the benefit? Why would employers want to jump through these hoops and be accountable for taking action on every candidate that they see?

Employer value prop

Save time

The improved matching helps us ensure that any candidate who reaches out through Indeed Connect is worth your while, with our UI built specifically to help you quickly glance through candidate qualifications and decide how to move forward.

Stand out

Indeed Connect jobs and the most responsive employers get special badging and are highlighted on both Indeed Connect as well as the regular site for better visibility. This is a free way for employers to stand out and get in front of the best candidates first.

Get (and make) a better impression

For highly competitive roles and markets, employers can engage with candidates who might be on the fence and help sell them on a position, converting candidates who might have otherwise passed on applying.

Similarly, employers can get an early indicator of things such as candidate engagement level and communication skills, which are both difficult to gauge from just a resume alone.

The process

How did we go about actually delivering these experiences.

Kickoff

We started things off with a pretty lean team of just a product manager, an engineer and myself. As resourcing and logistics were worked out, my goal was to use this time to dig into the wealth of existing research we had at Indeed around our personas, the problems they face and prior explorations on any similar ideas in the past. Building research summaries about these learnings into more easily consumable highlights for my counterparts helped focus some of our early planning.

It also  became even more useful as our team grew, since it gave new joiners a single, consolidated resource to review during their onboarding, helping them get up to speed.

Alignment

As we moved from initial market research and evaluation to building, my goal was to try and help make sure we had a shared understanding of who are target users were, and the core problems we were trying to solve.

We had a wealth of persona research to draw on and quickly aligned on Hiring Managers as our best option.

The main challenge was to nail down the various user problems we've discussed amongst the leads, but haven't yet consolidated into anything more tangible.

In this case I decided to do this by riffing on the classic value proposition canvas. Listing out the different Tasks, Gains and Pains our target persona faces while hiring on Indeed; followed by a second exercise focusing on coming up with solutions to these problems.

These clusters then lead into a list of needs statements, coupled with high level solutions.

Planning

This helped PM start to better articulate the core value propositions of our product, and drive some discussions on prioritization. In addition, breaking the high level solutions down to a more specific set of features and existing implementations had two benefits.

• It helped engineering start some of their investigations and call out potential risks to our timeline early on.
• It helped highlight potential dependencies we had on other teams.

These solutions would later be turned into epics in JIRA, with the needs statements being part of the UX requirements, as we continued to break the work down into further chunks over time.

Iterating

There were two high level approaches we were evaluating early on.

Job centric approach

This first approach focused more on surfacing job level entry points and putting job management and communication at the core of the experience.

User account centric approach

The second approach focused more on building a "Responsiveness Dashboard", emphasizing things like reputation scores, response times and gamification.

After reviewing amongst ourselves and with the executive team, we decided to focus on the first approach. A focus on jobs made more sense as a starting point for a few reasons.

1) While we wanted to incentivize responsiveness and good behaviour, this should be a result of effective job management and staying on top of your messages - not the main focus itself.

2) This approach allowed us to integrate and anchor our entrypoints in the UI off the existing jobs management page, while the dashboard would have been a whole new product space we'd have to build.

As we started converging on a solution, our iterations became more focused, exploring different variations on our core concepts. The shortlisted ones, we decided to put in front of users for some early testing and validation.

Test

The things we wanted to evaluate at this stage were the following:
Messaging and Value Props: Were employers fully understanding the features and benefits of the program? Were they understanding the 3 day responsiveness requirement?
Task completion: Were they able to actually complete the job setup, and then respond to new messages and applicants?

We learned the following:

“Everything seems thoroughly thought out and well designed. It’s got a good flow to it, there was nothing I was questioning. Nothing seemed to be lacking, it was all very clear.” – Research participant

Adjust

To address the above feedback, we adjusted the designs the following ways:

Added an additional step to confirm employers have read and understood the program conditions before turning the feature on.

Added additional "free" badging to different entrypoints.

Broke each category of questions out into distinct steps, and added additional helper text to provide more transparency on how information from each question will be used.

Build

To get our MVP out the door on time meant that engineering had to start as soon as possible, and would happen in parallel tracks with design.

As shown above, a lot of our early explorations focused on nailing down things like information architecture, general layout structure and task flows in order to allow the dev team to begin building out the necessary backend systems and APIs first, giving design additional time to test, iterate, converge and dial in specs.

During this process our engineers would often have new feedback, which would be incorporated into the designs, alongside additional stakeholder and user testing feedback.

The challenge with this approach was that design lead time often tended to be relatively short (2~ sprints generally). The upside was that it meant there was relatively little time wasted on building high fidelity mockups and detailed specs for things that would end up being de-scoped.

And since engineers were involved with a design from the beginning, there was a greater sense of ownership, and a better understanding of the designs, requiring less reviews from UX during implementation and QA.

The other challenge was co-ordinating with outside teams who owned systems and product areas we relied on, or in some cases would be contributing to. As in any major company, these teams would often have their own OKRs, contribution processes, design feedback or ongoing code migrations that we had to work with. So getting buyin, building these relationships and getting their teams involved early was crucial to our own success as well.

Conclusions

As with any new experiment, we took our time ramping up traffic, progressively increasing the percentage of users we exposed the service to over time. Below are a few of our outcomes, lessons and next steps as of the writing of this case study.

Takeaways

There's been a lot of lessons but some of the high level ones are:

Highlight dependencies and begin collaborations early
I touched on this above. We did a good job with some teams, but could have done better with others. A lack of control amplifies risk exponentially. While we were able to adjust designs and roadmaps accordingly, managing dependencies and delivering work that overlapped with so many different areas was one of the bigger challenges of this project.

Give people a voice. Help them help you.
That being said -  we're all on the same team. When reaching out to other teams for collaboration, making sure that they feel heard, and figuring out a way our work can benefit both sides often went a long way in getting early buy in.

Design by its nature is also a collaborative process, and as designers, we're often more used to giving and receiving feedback than folks in other disciplines. Taking the lead in driving these discussions can help teams align and move forward more quickly.

Cross timezone collaboration can be hard
We had team members spread across APAC, Europe and US timezones. This caused some growing pains at the beginning, but here are some things we did that helped streamline the process:

• Consolidate sources of truth, documenting everything from discussions to decisions.

• Build in time for async feedback. Make it a habit to follow up on any loose threads. If a question doesn't get resolved in 3~ days online, team calls and syncs should be regular enough to help address it there.

Don't let perfect be the enemy of good – but don't ship something you're not prepared to live with
Bit cliche, but it's always easier to iterate on an existing solution. Better to get something out there and then build and refine over time, than to have half a year pass you by and ship nothing.

That being said, try to make sure you're not painting yourself into a corner down the line. Temporary solutions have a way of sticking around, and can come bite you later. I've been down this road before so I was mindful of it when making decisions for our team, but was still reminded along the way - one of the teams we worked with ran into significant delays with a refactor that also blocked us. We were able to help them along, but it added about 3 weeks of development time on our end we didn't initially plan for.

Next Steps

Now that our MVP is out in the wild our main focus is on:

Measure and expand
We piloted our MVP with a few specific jobs such as Nurses, Line Cooks and about 10 other job categories in the US. Our goal is to continue ramping up our program, measuring and improving our matching algorithms and the quality of the data we capture, eventually expanding to most if not all jobs internationally.

Improve employer response times and messaging
We started with a blanket "respond in 3 days" but we have more recent research and data to indicate employers would be comfortable with much tighter requirements under the right conditions. Our ultimate goal is to help get all employers on Indeed Connect to respond within one business day, and getting there will be a major initiative for 2023.

Building the mobile app
This will help decouple us from some of the legacy issues we had to navigate with the web based experience, and help build a better messaging experience for employers (which should also tie in with the response time improvements above).
